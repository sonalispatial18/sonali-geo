How did urbanisation unfold in the Indian Western Himalaya over two decades?

Land use and land cover change mapped across Dharamshala and Pithoragarh (2002–2021)

The Planning Challenge

Himalayan mountain cities are among the most rapidly urbanising landscapes in South Asia. Yet planning decisions on road corridors, drainage, forest buffers, and settlement expansion are often made without spatially explicit, locally calibrated evidence of what was being lost and how fast.

The problem is compounded by scale: previous remote sensing studies of Himalayan urbanisation focused almost exclusively on large cities. Medium-sized towns — where the UN projects most future urbanisation will occur — were a blind spot. No one had mapped, at high resolution and across multiple time points, how the landscape of a Himalayan hill station was actually being restructured by tourism, road investment, and population pressure.

This study addressed that gap with a rigorous, two-decade, multiscale land cover record built entirely from open-access satellite data to support spatial framework for sustainable landscape development in mountain cities.

Methodology

Study sites: Two contrasting rapidly urbanising landscapes in the Indian Western Himalaya were selected to ensure findings were transferable across governance and topographic contexts:

  • Dharamshala, Himachal Pradesh (311 km² study area) — a tourism- and policy-driven hill station with linear valley corridor growth, designated India’s first Smart City in its state (2015) and winter capital of Himachal Pradesh (2017).
  • Pithoragarh, Uttarakhand (90.74 km² study area) — a border district headquarters with radial sprawl constrained by ridgelines, ranked first for population and household growth among similar altitude towns in Uttarakhand.

Classification: Multi-temporal high-spatial-resolution satellite imagery (Sentinel-2A at 10 m, ASTER at 15 m) was acquired at four time points spanning 2001–2021 and processed using maximum likelihood classification (MLC) with stratified random sampling validation (250 ground reference points per map). Classification accuracy ranged from 89.64% to 95.31% across all maps.

Rather than collapsing mountain vegetation into generic “forest / non-forest” bins, the classification resolved 12 ecologically meaningful land cover classes for Dharamshala — Deodar, Oak, Pine, Oak Mixed, Pine Mixed, Mixed Forest, Vegetation, Glaciated Region, Cropland, Open Area, Barren Land, and Built-up — and 8 classes for Pithoragarh. This species-level resolution makes the outputs usable for biodiversity corridor planning and ecosystem service modelling, not just urbanisation monitoring.

Analysis framework: Three complementary methods were applied:

  1. Multitemporal LULC mapping at city and sub-watershed scales — capturing both compact core dynamics and sprawl into outer zones

Tools: Google Earth Engine · ArcGIS · QGIS · ENVI · CARTOSAT-DEM · Bhuvan geo-platform

Key Findings

Scale of expansion

  • Built-up area increased by 104% in Dharamshala and 387% in Pithoragarh over the two decades — a near-fivefold expansion in the smaller city.
  • The steepest acceleration in both cities occurred during the second decade (2010–2021), coinciding with road infrastructure investment, tourism growth, and government-directed urban designation decisions.

What land was lost

  • In Dharamshala, urban expansion consumed cropland first, followed by vegetation and forest. Forest, vegetation, and cropland lost 1%, 2%, and 17% of their respective areas.
  • In Pithoragarh, urban expansion targeted cropland followed by vegetation, with forest, vegetation, and cropland losses of 15%, 11%, and 15%.
  • Cropland bore the primary conversion cost in both cities — the agricultural buffer between urban cores and forested slopes was the first and most actively targeted land class.

Growth morphology diverged by topography

  • Pithoragarh evolved as a single-core expansion, with built-up patches connecting and aggregating in all directions from the city centre — a continuous, radial sprawl pattern.
  • Dharamshala exhibited multi-core network expansion — with scattered newer built-up patches emerging beyond city limits, particularly in the western direction, interlaced with cropland and forest fragments.

Planning Inputs & Real-world Impact

This study produces decision-ready spatial intelligence across five planning domains:

1. Urban growth boundary definition Two decade long trjectory identifying which periods and which regions are most actively targeted — provides the empirical baseline for defensible, spatially grounded urban growth boundaries. This evidence directly supports municipal masterplan revision and boundary setting.

2. Forest protection planning Species-level forest class maps (Deodar, Pine, Oak, mixed types) pinpoint exactly where specific forest types have been lost or fragmented by urban expansion. The accelerating forest loss during Dharamshala’s high-growth decade is a measurable early warning signal for conservation planning and buffer zone demarcation under India’s Forest Conservation Act.

3. Slope stability and disaster risk screening Cropland-to-built-up conversion on steep Himalayan terrain is a leading indicator of landslide and flood exposure. Class-level change maps can be overlaid with slope, aspect, and drainage data to flag high-risk expansion zones before construction permits are issued — shifting from reactive disaster response to spatial pre-screening.

4. Ecosystem service accounting and trade-off analysis Species-level forest data and cropland loss estimates feed directly into carbon sequestration, flood regulation, local climate regulation, and soil erosion models (see the Ecosystem Services project). Planners can now quantify what ecosystem services are being traded away with each land conversion decision — a critical input for environmental impact assessments (EIA) and municipal-level SDG reporting.
